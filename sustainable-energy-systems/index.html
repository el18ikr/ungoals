Introduction

This section explores how technology is being used to build sustainable energy systems. The subject of climate change arouses passionate political debate, but the scientific evidence continues to show carbon in the atmosphere is of serious concern. This lesson will focus on how renewable energy sources can be integrated into a smart grid system in order to reduce our reliance on fossil fuels. To make this change requires engineers from many disciplines to collaborate and push technologies to their limits. Advanced chemical engineering is required for new fuels and energy storage systems, and so the final section will explore advanced materials for green energy.

Climate science is a specialist field encompassing many fields of expertise. The British Antarctic Survey, for example, based in Cambridge but carrying out surveys in Antarctica and the Arctic, conducts research into atmospheric science, climate change, ice movement, geoscience, ecosystems and oceanography. One of their key activities is to use piloted and autonomous aircraft that are fitted with gravimeters, magnetometers, ice-penetrating radar, and LIDAR to make measurements of the ice from the air. Another key experiment is to drill as far down as 1000m into the ice to take samples, which are then analysed to determine the amount of CO2 in the air bubbles. By drilling deep, they are measuring the CO2 levels from up to 2000 years ago, since the ice has built up from snowfall over many years. Their most recent data, shown in the figure below, confirms that CO2 levels rose considerably after the industrial revolution.

The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change is the UN organisation set up to study climate change science. Nearly 200 countries are members and more than 800 experts contributed to its Fifth Assessment Report, published in 2014. The Sixth Assessment cycle is published in 2021/22. The IPCC’s current conclusion is that global warming is likely (high confidence) to reach 1.5°C between 2030 and 2052 if it continues to increase at the current rate. They have high confidence that impacts have already been observed on natural and human systems and they have made various predictions based on different levels of CO2 emissions reductions.

Electricity generation continues to be heavily dominated by fossil fuels. There are methods for reducing CO2 from fossil fuel plants and processes, including carbon capture and storage (CCS). The other part of the solution is to use renewable energy sources, including wind, wave, tidal, solar, biofuels and geothermal systems. They are time varying, however, and like most natural resources, different parts of the globe are richer in some renewable sources than others.

The electricity distribution network has already evolved to integrate these diverse energy sources together, in order to average out the supply and demand variations and enable trading of energy across borders.  It is very challenging, though, to realise these sophisticated gigawatt-level power systems and the associated storage that is required. National grids already use large-scale hydroelectric storage systems to provide generating capacity in times of high demand or as a backup for other forms of generation.

Smart grid systems are electricity distribution networks that are much more dynamic than traditional ones. They make extensive use of information and communications technology such as 5G and IoT in order to control the network and integrate smaller, more scattered, renewable energy sources. In a smart grid system, the storage capacity is provided by many smaller-scale energy storage devices including batteries, flywheels and hydrogen fuel cells. The batteries in electric vehicles can even be integrated into the system. 

Energy storage and energy conversion are major aspects of advanced materials for green energy and will be explored at the end of the lesson. Battery technology, fuel cells, carbon capture and storage will be studied. These require advanced technologies including nanostructured materials. For example, quantum dots and nanowire arrays have been proposed for lithium‐ion batteries. The unit will conclude by highlighting some key materials research activities that could have a profound impact in the green energy area.

The energy mix

Electric power can be generated from fossil fuels, nuclear power, renewable energy sources and biofuels. The energy mix in different countries varies considerably depending on the local natural resources, regulatory framework, time of year, weather and demand. As a snapshot of total global demand and the energy mix, the charts below show the figures for 2017: The total primary energy use by sources (based on BP Statistical Review of World Energy) and the global electricity generation by source from the International Energy Agency, both of which provide very extensive data. “Other” includes waste, geothermal, tidal and other sources.

IEA: World Electricity Generation in TWh by Source (2017)

Primary energy from BP Statistical Review of World Energy

What do the numbers mean?

One TWh is a 1012 watt-hours or one billion kWh. The average annual electricity consumption for a home in Europe is 3,600kWh. One million tonnes of oil equivalent (mtoe) is 11.63 TWh according to the IEA definition. As a guide to the scale of these graphs, the total electricity figure for coal of 9863 TWh is enough to power 2.74 billion of the average European homes. Of course, energy consumption varies dramatically across the world and developed countries generally use a lot more than developing countries.

Fossil fuels

As the 2017 energy mix illustrates, while renewables attract all the attention for sustainable energy systems, making fossil fuel generation cleaner could have an enormous environmental impact. Natural gas and coal-fired power stations can reduce their carbon footprint through carbon capture and storage (CCS) and the use of biofuels. These are discussed in the sections on Advanced Materials for Green Energy.

The UK has moved away from coal generation in order to meet its carbon targets and only has 3 coal plants left as at May 2020. The snapshot of the UK’s energy mix shown below is on one of the UK’s record 47 successive coal-free electricity generation days in 2020. It shows, however, a heavy reliance on natural gas, which the UK mostly imports by pipeline from Norway, the Netherlands and Belgium and by sea from the USA, Russia and Qatar amongst others [www.gov.uk  > National Statistics Energy Trends: UK gas].

Drax power station near Leeds is one of the largest power plants in the UK, generating almost 4 GW (35TWh maximum energy per year), and can use a mixture of coal and biomass. They have conducted trials of peat-coke biomass but now use sustainable forest biomass. This is in the form of compressed wood pellets which comprise 75% of the fuel. Drax now produces 17% of the UK’s renewable energy. Biomass requires the sources (e.g. forests) to be carefully managed and independently verified in order to ensure they are truly sustainable. The market for biomass fuel needs to be profitable, attractive to investors, and yet carefully regulated, requiring careful government legislation.

Carbon capture and storage

The most common form of CCS is to capture the CO2 and burying it deep underground in a form that won’t escape (sequestration), but it can also be re-used in the production of certain products (video). The BECCS pilot project (BioEnergy with Carbon Capture and Storage) run at Drax was a world first, combining CCS with a 100% biomass feedstock. This trial was conducted with the firm C-Capture, which was span-out from the School of Chemistry at the University of Leeds in 2009. They use a patented solvent-based method to capture CO2 from the power station flue gas [video2). Biomass with CCS is important because it is a renewable fuel that offers the same advantages of inertia and frequency control as coal.

Hydroelectric

Hydroelectric power can be based on natural water flow (rivers and tides) and natural or man-made reservoirs. The Three Gorges Dam across the Yangtze River is the largest system in the world and can generate up to 22.5GW. The enormous Snowy scheme in Australia has more than 140 miles of aqueducts and tunnels. It has typically 1,600 gigalitres of useable stored water and can generate more than 4GW peak power output – more than most nuclear power plants, but not continuously. Snowy 2.0 was recently announced, to give more capacity, but some critics are concerned about its environmental impact. Pumped hydroelectric systems are important for energy storage

Nuclear

Nuclear power divides opinion and public approval has declined significantly since Chernobyl and Fukushima. Nevertheless, because of the carbon issue it has had something of a renaissance. There are more than 440 power reactors operational in the world and 54 under construction. The USA has the largest nuclear capacity by a significant margin, at 100TW, followed by France and China at around 60TW each.  In the USA, no new reactors were built for 30 years – following the Three Mile Island accident, many projects were cancelled. In the USA there has been a big upsurge in shale gas use recently, enabled by new hydraulic fracturing technology and horizontal drilling. This has changed the economics of nuclear power, but two reactors are under construction and more planned.

Wind and solar

Wind turbines and photovoltaic solar power have the best prospects globally, while wave, tidal, hydroelectric and geothermal systems can have special importance locally in certain countries. The first electricity generation with a wind turbine is attributed to Professor James Blyth, a Scottish electrical engineer and academic at Anderson’s College, who powered the lights in his holiday home in 1887. Wind power growth has increased very rapidly since 2001 and now accounts for over a thousand TWh, but still accounted for less than 5% of the global electricity generation in 2017. Solar only accounted for <2% but has great prospects for growth.

The wind energy harvested is proportional to the turbine area × wind speed cubed, plus wind speed increases with height above the ground. The trend is, therefore, for larger and larger turbines, with Vestas producing, for example, one with a 166m tower and 162m rotor diameter that is rated at 5.6 MW and incorporates condition monitoring sensors, de-icing, lightning detection and fire suppression. Hornsea One, off the Yorkshire coast in the North Sea, is currently the largest offshore wind farm in the world and can power one million homes. It spans more than 400 km2 and has 174 turbines, giving 1.2 GW capacity.

By way of comparison, the typical solar irradiance at sea level is approximately 1kW/m2 at noon on a clear day. With photovoltaic (PV) cells operating at circa 20% efficiency, to also generate 1.2 GW power (peak) would require solar panels covering an area of 2.5km × 2.5km (6.25 square kilometres). The Noor Abu Dhabi Solar Power Project in Abu Dhabi, UAE, has 3.2 million solar panels covering 8 square kilometres (as of 2020) and produces almost 1.2 GW, with an estimated cost of USD870 million.

One might wonder how many km2 would be needed to meet the world’s 2017 total consumption of 25710 TWh (from IEA): If the Noor Abu system gives 1.2 GW, but for an average of 5 hours per day, then you need:

25,710,000 (GWh) / [1.2 (GW)  x 365days x 5hours per day] =  11,740 such installations.

This equates to an approximate area of 94,000 km2

As a benchmark, the area of the Sahara Desert is 9.2 million km2

The cost-per-m2 of solar panels is, therefore, of major importance in determining the scale to which solar PV can be used. Some of the technologies that can be used to manufacture novel low-cost PV cells are explored later in this lesson. Another option is concentrated solar power (CSP) in which reflectors and/or lenses are used to focus sunlight from a large area to heat a fluid and the heat is used in a thermal electricity generation system.

The roofs of buildings offer a convenient location for solar panels. The Energy Saving Trust has a domestic solar energy calculator that estimates a typical UK installation would generate 2,764 kWh in one year. Domestic PV systems can be net contributors to the grid, saving money on bills, but currently cost typically £5000 including installation. Many companies have developed solar PV tiles, however, so the capital cost of PV may be significant lower for new builds.

Carbon footprint of renewables

As a final note on renewable energy sources, it is important to be aware of the embodied carbon: that is, the carbon footprint of the entire manufacturing, construction and operation chain must be analysed as part of the design in order to achieve an optimal system.

Electric vehicles

Comparing the world energy mix charts, you can see that oil consumption is the highest overall, but very little of the consumption is for electricity generation. Switching vehicles from gasoline and diesel fuel to electric is an important step but places additional demands on the grid system. Electric vehicles receive a lot of attention in the press these days, but it is not widely known that the electric car was already a popular form of transport up until the 1930s. Robert Anderson, a Scottish inventor built an electric carriage in around 1839 but it didn’t have rechargeable batteries.

The availability of petrol and cheap mass-produced cars with internal combustion engines led to a rapid decline in EVs. The Waverley Coupe introduced in 1910 cost $2250., while the Ford Model T was $900 and as discussed in Unit 1, the price dropped rapidly as production was scaled up and went below $300.

Vehicle-to-grid

Interest in EVs has now returned in order to improve air quality in cities and move to sustainable energy systems. A rapid growth in demand for EV charging points is one of the reasons for adopting Smart Grid technology. Shell Group has a division called NewMotion that is dedicated to smart charging solutions. NewMotion was founded in 2009 and acquired by Shell in 2017. It has the largest charging network in Europe. In 2018 they conducted a trial of a vehicle-to-grid (V2G) system in Amsterdam in collaboration with Amsterdam Smart City. The purpose of a V2G system is to integrate EV batteries into the grid as distributed energy storage: During periods of low demand (e.g. night time) the batteries are charged as normal; when the demand on the network is high, the batteries of parked cars feed energy back into the grid. The expected growth in EVs then becomes part of the solution of managing peaks and troughs in electricity demand. This is sufficiently effective for energy companies that, in 2020, EDF ran a promotion offering 8000 miles per year free electricity for drivers buying or leasing a car or van compatible with their V2G system (https://www.edfenergy.com/electric-cars/vehicle-grid).

The inconvenience of charging EVs is already being addressed by some companies using wireless power transfer (WPT). WiTricity, in the USA, is a leader in wireless EV charging with a patented high-efficiency technique developed at Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT) by Professor Marin Soljačić and his team. WiTricity now has more than 900 patents granted worldwide. Antennas for WPT can be embedded under the surface of the road, for example at bus stops, in parking spaces or traffic lights, to give seamless charging on the road – referred to as charge in motion by some companies. The Korea Advanced Institute of Science and Technology (KAIST) demonstrated the award-winning online electric vehicle (OLEV) for cars and buses in 2010 and has proposed it for trams and trains. ABB demonstrated a trolleybus system in 2013 called TOSA (Trolleybus Optimisation Système Alimentation). Bombardier have installed their PRIMOVE charging system in a number of cities.
