  2. Raw materials and ethical sourcing

The earth’s mineral reserves and fossil fuels are finite resources and how they are managed is of tremendous importance. The “tragedy of the commons” is one of the earliest attempts to explore sustainability from a social and ethical perspective. This example of cows grazing on open access land is attributed to the economist William Forster Lloyd who lectured on the subject in 1833 and ecologist Garrett Hardin who published his paper with that title in Science in 1968. The point of the example is to show that if a piece of land used for grazing is shared and not managed properly then there is no motivation for the cow owners to look after it and it will end up with no grass.

The conditions of miners in some countries are appalling and the value of raw materials in some countries can lead to bribery and corruption. The situation where a country and its people end up worse off because of such issues is referred to as the resource curse. One of the causes is that if an economy is heavily reliant on a few minerals, for example, then the economy becomes very vulnerable to their price on the commodities markets.

Also, the resources tend to be owned by a small minority (royals, landowners, and even former colonialists), and the income generated is often taken offshore and invested in assets like luxury properties rather than re-invested in the country. With all the wealth owned by a small minority, often for historical reasons, countries can end up with authoritarian or corrupt governments and ordinary people suffer greatly. In the most extreme cases, the tensions can lead to genocide. Fortunately, international action, including the efforts of the UN and many NGOs, is starting to bring many of these regimes to an end. At the time of writing, the UN had 13 peacekeeping operations around the world [4].

China has invested heavily in Africa as part of its Belt and Road Initiative [5] and it seems to be on favourable terms, with new airports, railways, communications networks, infrastructure projects, and factories all creating jobs. There are more than 10,000 Chinese companies operating in Africa. Critics, however, claim that the countries will be debt-laden for years and use the phrase neocolonialism.

If you would like to learn more about these issues you should read these two newspaper opinion pieces [6, 7] and, since the world of geopolitics is so changeable, do your own research and discuss your opinions in the forum.
