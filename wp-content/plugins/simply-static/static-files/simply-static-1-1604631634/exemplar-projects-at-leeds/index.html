Agriculture, healthcare, energy and sanitation in developing countries can all benefit from technologies such as IoT, robotics and renewable energy systems. An important theme of the discussions is to recognise that the infrastructure and skills training may not be sufficient to support the adoption advanced technology solutions and that simple solutions are often far more useful.

In this video, Emily Cummins, who was a Leeds University undergraduate student studying Management and Sustainability, tells the story behind the invention of one such solution – the solar fridge:

Giving support to developing countries is a very high priority for the UN. At present, a lot of the work is carried out through government aid, by NGOs and charities. Engineers from all disciplines can make major contributions to the SDGs and you have seen example projects from Leeds in agriculture, sanitation, energy and healthcare. The limited local infrastructure and skills means simple solutions are often best. Once the basic needs are met, investment can accelerate on providing internet connectivity and a quality education.

The Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation has the strapline ‘All Lives Have Equal Value’ and prioritises investment in healthcare and education as the key pathway out of poverty. Whilst generous donations from internet entrepreneurs are very important, the best long-term solution is to harness the profit motive so that businesses are encouraged to invest in the infrastructure and skills needed to deliver lasting change. In the next lesson you will explore how engaging corporations in sustainable development might be achieved, so that charity is no longer needed.

The following examples of projects at Leeds are funded by a range of sources.

Digitalisation of agriculture

The digitalisation of agriculture, also known as precision agriculture or smart farming, refers to the application of IoT, robotics, AI and big data to farming. To give a flavour of the techniques that can be used, the paper “Feeding the World With Microwaves: How Remote and Wireless Sensing Can Help Precision Agriculture” describes how sensors and satellite and drone-based multi-wavelength imaging can be used to monitor crops, minimise water use and optimise land use.

The N8 is a network of the 8 most research intensive universities in the North of England: Durham, Lancaster, Leeds, Liverpool, Manchester, Newcastle, Sheffield and York. The aim of the N8 AgriFood programme (https://www.n8agrifood.ac.uk/) is to harness the collective expertise of the 8 institutions to tackle the challenge of food security regionally, nationally and internationally with partners that include industry, government, SME’s NGO’s and charities.

The food security challenge is tackled across three research themes:

  • Sustainable food production
  • Resilient supply chains
  • Improved consumption and health

In one project, Dr Ali Zaidi from the School of Electronic and Electrical Engineering at Leeds is working with partners in Pakistan on IoT-based automation in the agriculture sector. IoT can support enhanced production with reduced human effort and more efficient use of water, pesticides and fertilizers. Wireless sensor networks (WSNs) have a vital role to play in precision agriculture (PA). PA uses specific sensors and software to ensure that the crops receive exactly what they need to optimize productivity and sustainability. PA includes retrieving real data about the conditions of soil, crops and weather from the sensors deployed in the fields. Crop fields are imaged by satellite or drone, and image processing is used to extract information and inform decisions. The figure below shows the system architecture of the WSN-based PA system. A wireless sensor network-based system monitors real-time crop health status. A low altitude drone is used to obtain multi-spectral images, which are analysed to classify healthy and unhealthy crops.

Medical technologies

Professor Pietro Valdastri, Director of the Leeds Institute of Robotics, Autonomous Systems and Sensing, is a world-leading surgical robotics researcher with expertise in endoscopy. In this video from the FutureLearn module ‘MedTech: AI and Medical Robots’ he explains his research into improved techniques for colorectal cancer screening using robotically assisted colonoscopy. A feature of the technique is that it can be used outside major hospitals.

His GCRF project aims to develop an ultra-low-cost endoscopy technique for gastric cancer screening in rural China. Gastric cancer and oesophageal cancers have the highest global mortality rates among other forms of cancer and more than one third of new cases worldwide are concentrated in China. In 2015 alone, more than one million people were diagnosed with this form of cancer in China and more than 870,000 died. Screening programmes reduce the mortality rate through early detection but are expensive and difficult to implement in low-resource settings and rural areas.

The team has developed a novel disposable, soft-tethered, swallowable, endoscopic capsule, with the aim of enabling cost effective gastroesophageal cancer screening in rural areas of low- and middle-income countries. Innovative aspects include water-jet actuation for mobility and visualization, a portable platform and disposable components. The miniature tethered “pill camera” facilitates a comfortable un-sedated cancer screening exam. It has a target cost per procedure of £2 and the capital cost of circa £400, compared with £80,000 for existing technology. The proposed platform is designed to fit in a single airline carry-on size suitcase with a total weight of just 10kg.

Sanitation

Professor Barbara Evans, Chair in Public Health Engineering in the School of Civil Engineering at Leeds is a member of the Water@Leeds interdisciplinary research centre. It brings together more than 100 researchers, working across the physical, biological, chemical, social and economic sciences, engineering and the arts. Her research is focused on water and waste water engineering, and water supply and sanitation in developing countries. She is Director of the Centre for Doctoral Training in Water and Waste Infrastructure Systems Engineered for Resilience (Water-WISER), run in partnership with Loughborough and Cranfield Universities.

There is a huge demand for new infrastructure investments in the global south; more than 4.4 billion people worldwide do not have a sanitation system that collects and treats all the waste produced by families properly, while 2.4 billion people urgently need new water supply services. Yet, the kind of water and sewage systems that developed countries use are too expensive to install in large areas, requiring innovative solutions.

Water-WISER aims to give graduates a solid training in the fundamental engineering and science of water and sanitation, solid waste management, water resources and drainage. This is combined with skills developments so that engineers can collaborate with non-engineers and non-scientists. This could mean working alongside and political scientists, city planners, designers, administrators, health specialists, and professionals working in international development and finance including development agencies such as the World Bank, UNICEF or WaterAid.

Microgrids

Prof Lovett leads the project “Creating Resilient Sustainable Microgrids through Hybrid Renewable Energy Systems”. This project focuses on energy distribution in off-grid communities to enable the development of sustainable and resilient energy distribution grids. It is working with rural communities of the low- and middle-income countries (LMICs) Tanzania, Uganda and Republic of the Congo, where currently at most 10% of the rural population has access to electricity. A scalable low-cost microgrid infrastructure is being developed that uses real-time operational strategies and sustainable energy generation. By integrating these at the system design stage, the solution aims to reduce the cost and increase sustainability.

This project includes researchers from Electronic and Electrical Engineering and Chemical and Process Engineering. However, rather than adopting very specialised engineering of the kind explored in “grid systems” in Unit 4, the project focuses on local needs and constraints. A truly sustainable and reliable energy distribution infrastructure needs to be designed based on actual needs and local realities, beyond a purely electrical performance perspective. For example, the system must take into account the modest local facilities for repair and maintenance. This will provide innovative distribution system architectures for the sustainable electrification of rural communities in the target countries.
